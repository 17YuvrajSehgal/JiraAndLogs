Reliability engineers detect anomalies routinely; what consumes most of their response time is identifying which past incident describes the current failure. Today's diagnostic systems sit at two design extremes: running every retrieval method on every window---accurate but expensive---or fixing a single pipeline that ignores the variety of evidence different incidents present. The choice is made once at deployment, not per window, and the cost of that single choice is both measurable and avoidable.

We present a capability-adaptive incident-triage agent that inspects each window's available evidence---numeric telemetry, structured logs, traces, Kubernetes events, or text alone---and assembles a per-window plan that invokes only the diagnostic methods the evidence warrants. The agent composes eleven retrieval and composition primitives, four on-demand evidence-fetchers, and a re-ranking step that consumes those fetchers' output. Every primitive invocation is cacheable; every decision is replayable from a per-window execution trace.

We evaluate on three datasets: synthetic microservices telemetry ($1{,}008$ test windows), the OpenTelemetry Demo ($247$), and real Apache Jira tickets ($304$). The agent matches the always-on cascade's retrieval quality (Hit@5 $= 0.758$ on synthetic) while saving $64.1\%$ of the dollar-equivalent cost at parity. Generalization to real production tickets yields the highest Hit@1 we observe ($0.782$). Two findings emerge that the framework's instrumentation makes detectable: the active evidence-gathering loop's positive contribution is not statistically distinguishable from zero (paired bootstrap $p = 0.18$), and one of its four tools is significantly harmful on synthetic data ($p = 0.002$). Page suppression converges to one paged ticket per incident on all three datasets.
