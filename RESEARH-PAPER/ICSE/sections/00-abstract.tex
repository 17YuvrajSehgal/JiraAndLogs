Production incidents drown on-call engineers in telemetry---logs, traces, metrics, Kubernetes events---but the real time sink isn't detection; it's \emph{recognition}. The fix almost always lives in a past Jira ticket a teammate has already written, yet finding it today is a manual keyword hunt that burns ten to thirty minutes per page and thousands of engineer-hours a year at enterprise scale. We turn the corporate Jira archive into a searchable institutional memory and present a capability-adaptive incident-triage agent that learns from past resolutions to surface the right ticket in seconds. For each monitoring window the agent inspects whatever evidence is available---numeric telemetry, structured logs, distributed traces, Kubernetes events, or text alone---and assembles a per-window diagnostic plan that invokes an expensive language-model verifier only when the cheap retrieval path is uncertain; every decision is replayable from a per-window execution trace. To enable rigorous evaluation in a space starved of paired data, we contribute two synthetic incident-triage benchmarks (Online Boutique, OpenTelemetry Demo) that for the first time pair the full telemetry stack---Loki logs, Tempo traces, Prometheus metrics, Kubernetes pod events---with humanized Jira tickets in a shared timeline schema. We then probe two distinct transfer regimes against the same agent: on the OpenTelemetry Demo it runs \emph{zero-shot} from its Online-Boutique-trained configuration and reaches Hit@5 = $0.756$; on $9{,}341$ real Apache Jira tickets across seven projects it learns under a \emph{family-held-out} split (train: Spark, Cassandra, HBase, Flink; held-out test: Kafka, MariaDB-Server) and surfaces the correct past incident as its top suggestion $95.8\%$ of the time, with Hit@5 reaching $1.000$ on the retrieval-evaluable subset---to our knowledge the first agentic-retrieval result reported on a real Apache Jira corpus. The agent matches the always-on retrieval baseline at $64.1\%$ lower wall-clock and dollar-equivalent cost, and converges to one paged ticket per incident on every dataset.
