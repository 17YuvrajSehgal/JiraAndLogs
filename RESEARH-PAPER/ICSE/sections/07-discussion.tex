\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The empirical work in \S\ref{sec:results}--\S\ref{sec:cross-app} produces five cross-cutting findings that the field's literature has not yet absorbed. We discuss each in turn and close with the design recommendations that follow from them.

\subsection{The on-demand evidence-gathering loop's null result}
\label{sec:discussion:react}

The active evidence-gathering loop is the agent's most architecturally ambitious contribution. The loop is fully implemented (\S\ref{sec:approach:tooluse}), fully instrumented with a five-mode failure-mode taxonomy, and fully replayable from per-window traces. The empirical result is that its positive Hit@1 contribution is not statistically distinguishable from zero on any of the three datasets we evaluated. The synthetic-microservices point estimate is $+0.0151$ at $p = 0.180$; the polyglot synthetic and real-Apache-Jira datasets each produce exactly zero per-window paired delta on every metric (\S\ref{sec:results:ablation},~\S\ref{sec:cross-app:doesnt}).

We report this as a finding, not as a failure, because it speaks to a broader open question about agentic retrieval. The conceptual appeal of on-demand evidence gathering is large enough that the term \emph{agentic retrieval} has begun to appear as a load-bearing claim in the AIOps and incident-response literature. The empirical question---whether the lift is real, and what its statistical envelope is---has received less attention than it should. Our three-dataset measurement under paired-bootstrap confidence intervals (\S\ref{sec:evaluation:stats}) is the most rigorous test the loop's lift has received in our corner of the literature, and the answer it returns is that the lift exists in point estimate, does not exceed the noise floor, and is not robust across applications. Future work that claims a tool-use lift should be expected to clear the same bar, on multiple datasets, with paired-delta confidence intervals.

The interpretation we draw is not that on-demand evidence gathering is doomed. It is that the loop's value is unlikely to come from a single one-size-fits-all token-overlap reranker over a fixed action set. The single statistically significant ReAct-related finding in our measurements is the \dsob{} extended-trace tool's \emph{negative} effect at $p = 0.002$ (\S\ref{sec:results:ablation}), with a mechanism (cross-family service-name tokens) that is intelligible and a remediation (per-family tool selection) that is implementable as a controller-rule edit. The natural follow-up is a controller that learns which tools to call on which scenario family from trace data accumulated in production, rather than the rule-based controller's flat allowlist for every active-fault window. The framework supports this extension as a single-component swap (\S\ref{sec:approach:extensibility}); the empirical question remains open.

\subsection{The false-novelty rate is language-model-induced, not architecturally inherent}
\label{sec:discussion:falsenovel}

The cleanest mechanistic finding of the paper is the sixty-fold collapse in the false-novelty rate when the language-model verifier is structurally disabled (\S\ref{sec:cross-app:falsenovel}). The two synthetic datasets that run the verifier produce false-novelty rates of $40.6\%$ and $39.7\%$; the real-Apache-Jira dataset that structurally skips the verifier produces $0.7\%$. The mechanism, localised by overlapping the false-novelty bucket with each individual novelty-signal's firing pattern, is the verifier's own novelty flag: on $94\%$ of false-novelty windows the verifier is the one signal of three whose output triggers the OR-composition.

The implication for system design is concrete. The agent's largest blind spot on the two telemetry-rich datasets is not architectural but configurational: the OR-disjunction over three novelty signals (the verifier flag, a retrieval-confidence threshold, and a learned classifier) trusts the verifier's flag at face value, and the verifier is over-eager. Two design changes follow from the finding, each one a small local edit. The first is to require two of the three signals to agree before emitting a novel decision: a soft-AND composition rather than an OR. The second is to apply the verifier-known-helpful gate from RQ-A8 (\S\ref{sec:approach:gating}) at the novelty-signal layer rather than only at the verifier-invocation layer: even on datasets where the verifier is helpful for retrieval re-ranking, its novelty flag can be selectively muted. The validation of either change against \dsob{} false-novelty is straightforward in our framework---rerun with a configuration switch, paired-bootstrap the delta---and we expect both to materially reduce the false-novelty rate by the mechanism the cross-dataset evidence identifies. Both are left to follow-up work because the finding is itself novel and the present paper's contribution is the discovery, not the remediation.

The broader interpretation is that single-dataset evaluations conflate \emph{caused-by-the-system} blind spots with \emph{caused-by-a-configurable-component} blind spots. A paper that evaluated the agent only on \dsob{} would have reported the $40.6\%$ false-novelty rate as an inherent property of the disjunction structure, recommended re-architecting the disjunction, and missed the actual cause. The cross-dataset replication is what surfaced the mechanism; the design recommendation is what follows from it.

\subsection{The cost-quality Pareto curve is favourable everywhere}
\label{sec:discussion:pareto}

The cost-savings claim (\S\ref{sec:results:cost}, \S\ref{sec:results:pareto}) is the most quantitatively confident result in the paper. The agent matches the always-on cascade's Hit@K at $64.1\%$ lower dollar-equivalent cost on \dsob{} and $65.3\%$ lower on \dsotel{}. The Pareto sweep across cheap-path-confidence thresholds from $0.50$ to $0.95$ is flat in Hit@K on both datasets: every threshold produces identical retrieval quality, varying only cost. The production recommendation that emerges is to drop the cheap-path threshold from its conservative documented default of $0.90$ to $0.50$, unlocking an additional four percentage points of cost savings at zero retrieval cost on \dsob{} (and a small additional gain on \dsotel{}).

The reason the Pareto is favourable across the entire operating range is identified by the skill-ablation results (\S\ref{sec:results:ablation}): on these datasets, the cascade-derived primitives the cheap-path gate skips when it is confident do not change rank-one rankings on the windows they are skipped from. The expensive primitives (the language-model verifier, the hybrid retrievers, the on-demand tools) contribute meaningful signal on a small subset of genuinely uncertain windows, and the cheap-path-confident gate is good at identifying that subset. The general lesson is that for diagnostic systems with strongly bimodal triage confidence distributions---most windows clearly noise, most active-fault windows clearly uncertain---a cheap-first / escalate-on-uncertainty architecture is the right structural pattern, and the cheap-path threshold is the most under-discussed cost knob in the literature.

A reviewer's natural objection is that the cost-savings number depends on a specific per-skill cost table (Appendix~A). The savings \emph{percentage} is invariant to model unit-rate, because both the actual and counterfactual scale together; the percentage is therefore portable across LLM providers, model versions, and self-hosted-versus-vendor configurations.

\subsection{The capability-adaptive design transfers}
\label{sec:discussion:portability}

The agent's structural claims---per-window plans, evidence-driven gating, replayable traces, cost-aware cheap-first / escalate-on-uncertainty---hold across three structurally different datasets without per-application tuning. The cross-dataset evidence (Table~\ref{tab:generalises}) is the single best support for the portability claim that the field's literature usually accepts on architectural-elegance grounds rather than empirical ones. Three numbers carry this finding: $1.000$ pages-per-incident on all three datasets, $64$ to $65$ percent cost savings on the two datasets where the comparison is well-defined, and the numeric-feature classifier's $p < 10^{-4}$ significance on triage accuracy on both datasets that carry numeric features. The capability-driven gate is not a paper-design abstraction; it is a mechanism that has been exercised on three datasets and that has shown the right behaviour on each.

The natural caveat is that the three datasets are not independent samples from a population of incident-triage workloads: \dsob{} and \dsotel{} share the lab construction discipline, and \dswol{} replaces the telemetry side with engineer-voice prose rather than a third synthetic application. We discuss the implications of this in \S\ref{sec:threats}; the cross-dataset reading we draw here is that the structural transfer claim is well-supported within the regime of telemetry-paired-with-Jira-style-text, and that extending it to qualitatively different operational regimes (cloud-native serverless workloads, edge-deployed mobile telemetry, mainframe operations logs) is a future-work claim rather than a present-paper claim.

\subsection{Honest reporting of statistically non-significant results}
\label{sec:discussion:honesty}

The paired-bootstrap statistical envelope (\S\ref{sec:evaluation:stats}) was a deliberate methodological choice rather than a defensive after-thought. The single-report bootstrap on the agent's Hit@1 has a $\pm 5\%$ absolute confidence-interval width on \dsob{} and $\pm 9\%$ on \dswol{}; most Phase-2 ablation deltas in our measurements are of order one to three percentage points, well below the single-report-CI width. Paired-bootstrap intervals are tighter because they exploit the per-window correlation between baseline and ablation arms, and are the only fair way to make a statistical claim about a small ablation delta on a small test split.

The pattern of significance our paired-delta CIs surface is informative beyond the agent-paper context. Of approximately eighty ablation cells we ran across the three datasets, six produce statistically significant findings (\S\ref{sec:results:ablation},~\S\ref{sec:results:capmask}): three independent re-derivations of the numeric-feature classifier's essentiality, two sanity-check capability-removal findings on real Apache Jira, and one negative finding on the extended-trace tool. The remaining cells produce exactly zero per-window paired delta, meaning every window scores identically across the ablation and the baseline. The honest empirical claim is that the cascade-derived primitives the agent draws from have largely interchangeable effects on the rank-one slot on these datasets: the BiEncoder anchor dominates, and the other primitives serve as backup signals on the few windows where it is uncertain. This is a structural finding about the dataset's retrieval-difficulty distribution, not a defect of the cascade or the agent. It is, however, a useful finding to publish because future-work designs that target rank-one improvements on similarly structured datasets will face the same statistical-power constraint and should plan their ablation budgets accordingly.

\subsection{Design recommendations}
\label{sec:discussion:recommendations}

The discussion above produces four design recommendations whose evidence sits directly in the paper.

\paragraphhead{Drop the cheap-path threshold to $0.50$ as the production default.} Recovers four percentage points of dollar-equivalent cost savings on \dsob{} at zero Hit@K cost; the same threshold-invariance pattern holds on \dsotel{}. The recommendation is dataset-specific in its absolute savings figure but architecturally portable.

\paragraphhead{Disable the extended-trace tool by default; enable it per scenario family.} The trace tool is significantly harmful on \dsob{} at $p = 0.002$. A controller policy that enables the tool only on scenario families where its service-graph context is dataset-validated to outweigh the cross-family token noise is the implementable follow-up. The framework supports this as a controller-rule edit (\S\ref{sec:approach:extensibility}).

\paragraphhead{Replace the novelty composer's OR-disjunction with a soft-AND that requires two of three signals.} Localises the false-novelty fix to the disjunction structure rather than to the verifier itself, and is expected to reduce the synthetic-dataset false-novelty rate by the same mechanism that produces \dswol{}'s sixty-fold collapse. The change is a one-line edit; the validation is a paired-bootstrap rerun.

\paragraphhead{Continue to require paired-delta confidence intervals on small ablation deltas.} The single-report bootstrap is too wide to surface most agent-ablation deltas; the paired-delta interval is what makes the per-cell finding reportable. This is a community-process recommendation rather than a system-design one, but it bears on every retrieval-or-triage paper that reports ablation tables on splits smaller than a few thousand windows.
