\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We addressed the diagnosis bottleneck in microservice incident response by treating Jira-as-memory: given a window of live telemetry, retrieve the past tickets that describe the most-similar prior incident, and flag the windows where no past ticket applies. We constructed a public, reproducible paired telemetry-Jira corpus under a strict no-label-leakage discipline; trained seven diagnostic pipelines representing different inductive biases on the joint problem; and composed them into a four-layer cascade---a stacked-logistic triage gate, a Reciprocal-Rank-Fusion retrieval layer with a multi-retriever overlap rerank, an OR-combined three-signal novelty layer, and a final output composition---that ties or beats every individual pipeline on every metric. On 1{,}008 held-out in-distribution test windows, the cascade achieves Hit@5 of 0.912, MRR of 0.794, strict triage PR-AUC of 0.9998, and novel recall of 0.793 at preserved precision, at sixteen microseconds per window after the per-pipeline predictions are cached.

The cascade transfers to the OpenTelemetry Demo, a structurally distinct polyglot application with Kafka-based asynchronous communication that the source application does not carry, under two reporting columns (a strict zero-shot column and a Layer-1-retrained column). The cascade's structural template generalizes completely (ninety-four out of ninety-four feature columns produced on the target); its value provenance is partial (twelve of ninety-four columns populate from application-shared signals, the rest depend on application-specific instrumentation), which we report as an honest structural-versus-value compatibility finding. The cascade degrades gracefully under memory-noise injection at distractor ratios up to fifty percent and under leave-one-family-out cross-validation, where novelty F1 sits at eleven percent relative below the in-distribution figure and novel recall still beats the locked baseline by 381\% relative.

Three independent reranker designs---a cross-encoder with a binary cross-entropy loss, a language-model agent's verification override, and a language-model judge with a strict JSON output---each underperformed the cascade's multi-retriever overlap rerank, isolating an operating regime in which multi-retriever consensus on the top-one slot is empirically harder to beat than any single-model judgment we tested. The same data reframes the research story: the project's headline win came not from improving Hit@5 (which sat at over ninety-three percent of the union ceiling of the chosen retrievers' top-five lists from the beginning) but from a small learned per-window classifier replacing a fixed retrieval-confidence threshold inside the novelty layer. Three directions stand out for future work. First, larger and real-Jira corpora: the synthetic-but-anchored humanized corpus we use should be replaced or augmented by tickets from cooperating organizations whose data-sharing constraints are compatible with external evaluation. Second, adaptive control flow at the cascade level: the current cascade is deterministic and feed-forward, but its expensive components (the language-model agent in particular) are needed only on the uncertain windows, and routing them adaptively would lower the dominant offline-inference cost without affecting the headline numbers. Third, cross-domain generalization beyond microservices: the cascade's separation of concerns---a triage gate, a retrieval fusion, and a novelty channel---is plausibly transferable to incident-response settings outside microservices (batch data pipelines, Internet-of-Things device fleets, multi-region database clusters), each of which would test a different axis of the structural-versus-value compatibility distinction the cross-application section identifies.
