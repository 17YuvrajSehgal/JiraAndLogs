\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\noindent\textbf{Triage-as-classification is the wrong yardstick.} The task most
automated-triage work optimizes, deciding whether a window is ticket-worthy at
all, is saturated where it is usually measured and barely learnable where it
matters: on injected faults an active fault is separable from a healthy baseline
almost by construction, while on real engineer-filed tickets every classifier we
tried sat at chance (\S\ref{sec:results-rq4}). Strong synthetic triage numbers
therefore certify the dataset, not the method, and a system tuned to maximize them
optimizes a quantity that does not transfer. The problem worth solving is the one
that does, recognizing \emph{which} past incident a live one resembles, and it is
far from saturated, which is where the gains we report come from.

\noindent\textbf{Method conclusions flip with scale and realism.} Had we evaluated
retrieval only on the synthetic corpora, as is common, we would have concluded
that fusing retrievers is useless, even mildly harmful, and shipped a single dense
model. On the real corpus, an order of magnitude larger and spread across two
dozen projects, the same fusion is decisively best (Figure~\ref{fig:forest}): in a
small, homogeneous memory one retriever dominates and weaker signals only add
noise, whereas a large, diverse memory creates the blind spots that complementary
retrievers cover. With only three corpora we cannot fully disentangle scale from
diversity, so we make the per-dataset claim: fusion is significantly best on the
large, heterogeneous real corpus and ties or trails the dense retriever on the
small synthetic ones. Retrieval comparisons thus need realistic scale \emph{and}
diversity before their conclusions can be trusted---one reason we release
real-scale data alongside the synthetic.

\noindent\textbf{Robustness comes from asking ``does this step help \emph{here}?''}
The right action depends on the deployment. The language-model verifier improves
synthetic triage yet is net-negative on real text and is switched off there; the
retrieval-augmented language model never beats the domain-tuned retriever whose
candidates it re-reads. A system that hard-codes such components pays for them
everywhere and is hurt where they misfire. Our controller instead treats both
\emph{what evidence is present} and \emph{whether an expensive step is actually
beneficial} as decisions made per window and per setting, which is what lets one
implementation run from full telemetry down to bare text, suppress duplicate
pages, and shed $40$--$78\%$ of per-window cost with no loss in accuracy
(Figure~\ref{fig:adaptive}). Large language models are not a default win in this
domain; the leverage lies in a controller that invokes them selectively and
measures their effect.

\noindent\textbf{The system's contribution is efficiency and reach, not peak
accuracy.} The full system does not beat the best single retriever on raw hit
rate; it \emph{matches} that quality while spending a fraction of the budget, and
it keeps working when most of the evidence disappears. In real operations, where
instrumentation is uneven, alert volume is punishing, and compute is metered,
peak-quality retrieval when it is warranted, a lightweight path when it is not,
and roughly one alert per incident are precisely the properties that make a triage
assistant deployable.

\noindent\textbf{Open problems.} Two gaps frame future work. First, the knowledge
graph supplies correct candidates the other retrievers miss yet moves top-five
recall little; richer relational extraction or a learned graph matcher, rather
than entity overlap, may convert that latent signal into measured gains. Second,
our real-world data is text only while our telemetry-rich data is synthetic: the
open frontier is real telemetry paired with real, human-written tickets at scale.
The datasets, harness, and baselines we release lower the barrier to that step;
\S\ref{sec:threats} discusses the limitations that temper these conclusions.
