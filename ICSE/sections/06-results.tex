\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{RQ1: Retrieval Quality}
\label{sec:results-rq1}

Table~\ref{tab:retrieval} reports retrieval quality across all three datasets.
Two findings stand out. First, a retriever fine-tuned on the task far
outperforms strong off-the-shelf methods: our dense bi-encoder beats every
zero-shot dense embedder, classical lexical search, a cross-encoder re-ranker,
and a retrieval-augmented LLM on all three datasets---on the real incidents, for
example, it reaches \hit{5}~$0.91$ against $0.83$--$0.87$ for the best baselines.
The retrieval-augmented LLM, despite re-reading the same candidates, never
surpasses the trained retriever, indicating that the gains come from
domain-adapted retrieval rather than from a general reader.

Second, the value of \emph{fusion} depends on the setting. On the large,
heterogeneous real corpus, fusing the dense, sparse, and graph retrievers lifts
\hit{5} from $0.91$ to $0.97$---placing the correct prior incident in the top
five $97\%$ of the time, at \hit{1}~$0.84$ and MRR~$0.90$. This gain is large and
statistically significant: against the dense retriever the paired-bootstrap
difference is $+0.065$ ($95\%$ CI $[+0.058,+0.073]$), and against the strongest
baselines it ranges from $+0.12$ (the LLM) to $+0.14$ (zero-shot dense), all with
Benjamini--Hochberg $q\approx 0$. On the two small synthetic corpora, by
contrast, the fine-tuned dense retriever is already so strong that fusion does
not help, and on Online Boutique it is slightly worse ($-0.076$, $q=0.03$):
when one retriever dominates a small memory, blending in weaker signals only adds
noise. Fusion's advantage thus \emph{emerges with scale and diversity}---precisely
the conditions of real operations. Figure~\ref{fig:forest} summarizes every
pairwise comparison with its bootstrap confidence interval and significance.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\columnwidth]{figures/fig_significance_forest.pdf}
\caption{Difference in \hit{5} between Hybrid-RRF and each alternative (paired

\label{fig:forest}

We further isolated the knowledge graph's contribution by comparing the full
fusion against fusion without it. The graph does surface correct candidates the
others miss---on $14\%$ of Online Boutique queries it retrieves the right
incident where the dense retriever fails---but in fusion this rarely changes
top-five recall (the $\pm$graph difference is not significant on any dataset).
The graph is therefore a real but marginal contributor: useful for top-rank
precision and coverage, not a driver of \hit{5}.

\subsection{RQ2: End-to-End Triage and Graceful Degradation}
\label{sec:results-rq2}

Table~\ref{tab:agent} reports the full agent. The headline is robustness across
evidence regimes: the \emph{same} system, with no reconfiguration, retrieves
strongly whether it has complete telemetry or only text. On the real, text-only
incidents it reaches \hit{5}~$0.96$---essentially realizing the hybrid retriever's
quality end to end---while on the fully instrumented synthetic systems it reaches
$0.76$. Page suppression works as intended: across all three datasets the agent
emits about one page per incident, collapsing the many windows of a single
incident into a single alert.

\subsection{RQ3: The Cost of Autonomy}
\label{sec:results-rq3}

What does the capability-gated controller buy? Running only the steps the
evidence supports, and escalating expensive steps only under uncertainty, cuts
per-window cost---wall-clock time and model usage---by $40\%$, $63\%$, and $78\%$
on the three datasets relative to a cascade that runs everything (Table~\ref{tab:agent}),
and the saving grows with scale, since larger workloads contain more windows the
cheap path can settle. Crucially, this thrift costs no accuracy: an ablation that
removes each component in turn moves \hit{1} by at most $0.012$, and sweeping the
evidence-gathering budget leaves \hit{5} unchanged. The same measurements carry a
candid message: because no single component is load-bearing, the heavyweight
evidence-gathering tools contribute little measurable \hit{} gain on these
datasets; their role is to keep cost low by running only when cheaper steps are
unsure, and the headroom they could add is bounded by how often retrieval is
already correct.

\subsection{RQ4: Synthetic Versus Real, and What It Reveals}
\label{sec:results-rq4}

Our most consequential finding concerns the triage \emph{classification} task
that dominates synthetic benchmarks---deciding whether a window is ticket-worthy
at all. On synthetic data it is trivially easy: standard classifiers over the
telemetry features reach a near-perfect area under the precision--recall curve and
are well calibrated (Figure~\ref{fig:triage}). On the real incidents, the very same
classifiers collapse to chance---an area under the precision--recall curve of
$0.51$ against a $0.50$ random floor, and an ROC area of $0.50$---and the agent's
own triage accuracy ($0.51$) tracks the trivial majority-class baseline. The
difficulty of synthetic triage is an artifact of fault injection, where active
faults are separable from baselines by construction; on real, engineer-written
tickets, whether an issue was ultimately deemed worth fixing is simply not
predictable from its text alone.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\columnwidth]{figures/fig_triage_reframing.pdf}
\caption{Triage classification (RQ4): a gradient-boosted classifier's PR-AUC and
the agent's triage accuracy. Synthetic triage is saturated (PR-AUC $\approx 1$);
real-world triage sits at the chance floor (majority-class baseline $0.495$). The
transferable difficulty lies in retrieval, not classification.}
\label{fig:triage}
\end{figure}

This reframes how operational triage should be measured: the part that looks
solved on synthetic data is the part that does not transfer, while the part that
does transfer---retrieving which past incident a live one resembles---is where the
real headroom lies, and where our system is strongest.

Because the real dataset's matches are inferred rather than human-labelled, we
checked their validity with an LLM-as-judge. Asked to rate the relevance of each
gold match against a random control, the judge separates them on every dataset,
and most sharply on the real incidents (a gold-versus-random gap of $1.24$ on a
five-point scale, versus $0.34$--$0.47$ on synthetic). The matches the real-world
retrieval numbers are measured against therefore carry genuine signal.
