\section{Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{Dataset and labels}
\label{sec:approach:dataset}

The workload is Google's Online Boutique~\cite{onlineboutique}, a ten-service e-commerce demo whose services are written in six languages and communicate over remote procedure calls with a cache backing the cart service. We forked the upstream code and added production-realistic telemetry under a single binding rule: \emph{anything a real production service would not emit must not be added}. Concretely, we reject any field that names our scenario taxonomy---no scenario identifiers in span attributes, no fault-type strings in log fields, no alert names tied to our incident families. The discipline makes debugging harder but the resulting telemetry indistinguishable from what an instrumented production fleet would emit, so the evaluation that follows describes the production task rather than a fictional easier surrogate.

Three telemetry channels are added to every service. The logging channel emits a structured record per remote-procedure call, plus a structured error record at every dependency boundary capturing what was being called, with what operation, with what error class, and at what retry attempt. A small set of business events records domain-level signal (cart-size changed, order placed, payment charged) without ever encoding scenario identity. The tracing channel attaches errors to the span where they occurred and adds manual child spans for dependency calls with standard semantic attributes. The metrics channel exports the rate, error, and duration of every handler, together with per-dependency client breakdowns and standard runtime gauges from each language's default exporter.

Faults are injected through four conceptual mechanisms that collectively define a taxonomy of 27 scenario families. \emph{Service removal} takes a chosen service offline; this backs the eight hard-outage families and the cart-dependency outage. \emph{Behavioral perturbation} changes the configuration of a still-running service (slowing it, biasing its responses, raising its traffic rate); this backs latency-injection and traffic-pressure scenarios. \emph{Pod-level disruption} terminates and replaces pods to model graceful redeploys, single-pod restarts within a healthy replica set, and pathological flapping. \emph{Network-level disruption} delays, drops, or partitions traffic between specific service pairs via a chaos-engineering tool. A fifth, trivial mechanism is passive observation under normal traffic, which produces the negative-class baseline windows.

Each fault produces an \emph{incident episode} with three associated \emph{telemetry windows}: a pre-fault baseline captured just before injection, an active-fault window during the injection itself, and a recovery window after the fault is cleared. Long-running scenarios such as slow memory leaks add a fourth, longer observation window. Every window carries five categories of label that downstream training and evaluation consume.

The first is the triage class, one of \emph{ticket-worthy}, \emph{borderline}, or \emph{noise}. We use three classes rather than two because forcing borderline cases into a binary task biases the evaluation; the third class lets us measure how the model handles genuinely ambiguous windows without penalizing it for refusing to commit. The second is, for ticket-worthy windows, the metadata the corresponding Jira ticket would carry: a severity (minor, major, or critical), the list of affected components, and a coarse reason class. The third is the memory-match ground truth---the list of past Jira tickets whose scenario family matches the window's, restricted to tickets time-visible at the window's timestamp and originating from a different fault-injection run so that the model cannot trivially recover the gold by recognizing its own run. A Boolean \emph{novelty} label fires when this list is empty. The fourth is a fixed-dimensional vector of pre-computed numeric features summarizing the window's logs, traces, metrics, and Kubernetes-state counters, including both absolute moving averages and active-versus-pre-fault deltas; these are the inputs the numeric-feature pipelines consume. The fifth is a free-text evidence summary that interleaves a one-line description of metric anomalies, a small set of characteristic log lines extracted by a baseline-versus-fault diff, and the names of any firing alerts; this text stands in for the engineer's view of the window when retrieving past tickets.

A separate 347-ticket humanized Jira memory corpus paired with the telemetry, plus a 110-ticket distractor pool used to test robustness, are described in \S\ref{sec:background:lab}. The 6{,}720 windows are split chronologically and family-stratified into train, validation, and test partitions; every scenario family appears in all three partitions via disjoint runs, modeling a mature team's operational view. A complementary leave-one-family-out cross-validation, in which an entire family is hidden from training and evaluation switched to that family alone, tests the harder orphan-family regime.

\subsection{Seven pipelines and the Pareto frontier}
\label{sec:approach:panel}

We train seven diagnostic pipelines, each representing a different inductive bias on the joint telemetry-Jira problem. A \emph{histogram gradient-boosted classifier} on the numeric features provides a strong triage signal but produces no retrieval output. A \emph{dense bi-encoder retriever}, built on a small pretrained sentence transformer and fine-tuned on (window, gold-ticket) pairs with a contrastive ranking objective, retrieves past tickets via cosine similarity in embedding space. Two \emph{hybrid sparse-plus-dense retrievers} fuse classical term-matching, dense embeddings, and a graph-similarity score via rank fusion; the two variants differ only in how the graph entities (affected services, observable symptoms, error classes, root causes, fixes) are extracted---one by hand-written regular expressions, the other by a large language model under a strict JSON schema. A \emph{small log-sequence transformer} trained from scratch on tokenized log lines captures patterns specific to characteristic scenario families. A \emph{knowledge-graph retriever} matches each query window against a graph populated from the language-model extraction by scoring incidents by entity overlap. Finally, a \emph{three-stage language-model agent} hypothesizes a root cause from the evidence alone, then verifies a small candidate set retrieved by the cheaper pipelines, emitting a structured judgment that includes a novelty flag whenever no candidate matches.

The pipelines are deliberately heterogeneous because no single one dominates the others. The numeric-feature classifier owns the triage axis, the dense retriever wins top-1 retrieval precision, the hybrid fusion wins top-5 retrieval coverage, the log-sequence transformer wins on specific scenario families, and only the agent emits novelty signal at all. The natural alternative---training a single larger model that learns the union of these signals---is unattractive: the cheapest pipeline already saturates triage at the noise floor of the dataset, and the input modalities are deeply heterogeneous (a numeric vector, free-text evidence, log token sequences, structured graph queries) so that a unified architecture would have to carry separate encoders for each. We therefore compose the seven into a cascade.

\subsection{Cascade architecture}
\label{sec:approach:cascade}

The Tiered Cascade Hybrid (\tch{}) is a four-layer composition organized by per-call cost: a fast triage gate (Layer~1) runs on every window, a retrieval fusion (Layer~2) consumes cached upstream predictions, a novelty check (Layer~3) consumes one cached agent output per window, and a final composition layer (Layer~4) assembles the result. The layers are deliberately independent: the novelty check never reorders the retrieval ranking, the retrieval layer never feeds back into triage, and the triage decision never gates retrieval. This independence is what makes per-layer ablation clean and what allows operators to swap any single pipeline without disturbing the others.

\paragraph{Layer 1: triage gate.} Layer~1 outputs a calibrated probability that the window is worth a ticket. It is a class-balanced logistic regression \emph{stacker} over the per-pipeline triage scores:
\begin{equation}
  P_{L1} \;=\; \sigma\!\left(\sum_{i} w_i\,x_i + b\right),
  \label{eq:l1}
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the triage score emitted by the $i$-th pipeline. The stacker is fit via out-of-fold cross-validation so that every training window is scored by a model that never saw it during fit, and class weights are balanced to compensate for the natural skew between ticket-worthy and noise windows. The numeric-feature classifier carries by far the largest learned weight; the remaining pipelines act as small corrections that pay off on borderline-class windows, where the numeric classifier alone is uncertain.

\paragraph{Layer 2: retrieval fusion.} Layer~2 produces a ranked list of five past tickets. It uses two different combination rules because the top-1 slot and the remaining four slots optimize different objectives: the top-1 slot rewards a confident consensus pick, whereas positions 2--5 reward diverse coverage. For the top-1 slot, we take the dense retriever's three highest-ranked candidates as an \emph{anchor pool} and re-rank them by how strongly the other retrievers concur,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{overlap}(c) \;=\; \sum_{r \in \mathcal{V}} \mathbf{1}[c \in \mathrm{top3}(r)]\,\bigl(3 - \mathrm{rank}_r(c)\bigr),
  \label{eq:l2-overlap}
\end{equation}
where the voter set $\mathcal{V}$ includes the two hybrid-fusion retrievers and the log-sequence transformer. A candidate that several independent retrievers agree on is more trustworthy than the dense retriever's own first guess; in the absence of any overlap, the dense retriever's top candidate is used unchanged.

For positions 2--5 we apply Reciprocal Rank Fusion~\cite{cormack2009rrf}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RRF}(c) \;=\; \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \frac{1}{60 + \mathrm{rank}_r(c)},
  \label{eq:l2-rrf}
\end{equation}
summed over a retriever set $\mathcal{R}$ comprising the dense retriever, one of the two hybrid-fusion variants, the log-sequence transformer, and the knowledge-graph retriever. Candidates are sorted by their RRF score; the anchor (already at position~1) is removed; the next four candidates fill positions 2--5. We chose Reciprocal Rank Fusion deliberately over learned or score-weighted alternatives: it requires no per-retriever score calibration and is invariant to monotone score transformations, which makes it robust to retrievers whose confidence distributions differ.

\paragraph{Layer 3: novelty check.} Layer~3 emits a single Boolean. It OR-combines three independent signals,
\begin{equation}
  \mathtt{is\_novel} \;=\; \mathtt{agent\_novel} \;\lor\; \bigl(\max_i \mathrm{conf}_i < 0.5\bigr) \;\lor\; \bigl(P_{\text{learned}}(\mathrm{novel}) \geq 0.5\bigr),
  \label{eq:l3}
\end{equation}
each of which captures a different reason the cascade might be wrong about a window having a past match. The first disjunct is the agent's own verify-stage judgment, expensive but specific. The second is a threshold over the maximum per-retriever confidence: if no retriever is even half-confident in any candidate, the cascade has effectively no past-match hypothesis, regardless of what the agent says. The third is a learned classifier over coarse window metadata---window type, scenario family, service name, a flag for intentionally-difficult cases, the maximum retrieval confidence, and the triage probability---trained again via out-of-fold cross-validation; it captures regularities the other two miss, such as that pre-fault baseline windows are novel by construction and recovery windows are not. We use disjunction rather than a weighted combination because the three signals capture qualitatively different forms of evidence and operators benefit from being able to tune a single learned-signal threshold without redesigning the others.

\paragraph{Layer 4: composition.} Layer~4 packs the calibrated triage probability, the ranked top-five past tickets, and the novelty flag into the final output and derives a coarse ticket-worthy-or-noise decision from the triage probability via the Layer-1 threshold. No further computation occurs.

The four-layer composition is what makes seven Pareto-incomparable pipelines compose into a system that beats every individual baseline on every metric. The layers do not interact: each is replaceable in isolation, each is ablatable in isolation, and the cascade's behavior on any given window can be traced layer by layer back to the upstream pipeline outputs it consumes.
