\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern cloud services emit a relentless stream of alerts, and the engineers on
call must decide, often within minutes, which alerts signal a genuine incident
worth investigating and which are noise. The decision is consequential in both
directions: a missed incident becomes an outage, while a flood of low-value
pages erodes attention and trust in the alerting system itself. What makes a
seasoned on-call engineer effective is rarely raw signal-reading; it is
\emph{memory}. Most incidents rhyme with something the team has resolved
before, and the fastest path from symptom to fix is to recognize the current
situation as ``the message-broker outage from last quarter'' and to reuse the
diagnosis that worked then. The institutional record of how past incidents were
resolved---typically a backlog of closed issue-tracker tickets---is therefore
the single most valuable triage aid a team owns. Yet it is largely untapped,
because finding the \emph{right} prior incident for a live one is itself a hard
retrieval problem.

Two assumptions, however, limit how far those results carry into practice.
First, evaluation is almost always conducted on \emph{synthetic} fault-injection
telemetry: faults are scripted into a demonstration microservice application, so
the resulting ``incidents'' are clean, balanced, and separable by construction.
Whether methods that excel on such data transfer to the messy, imbalanced,
free-text incidents that engineers actually file is rarely tested. The problem
is partly one of data: there is no public corpus that pairs operational
telemetry---logs, metrics, and traces---with the incident tickets and labels
these systems are meant to connect, so each study rebuilds its own data and
seldom releases it, hampering both reuse and head-to-head comparison. Second,
systems are typically designed for a single, rich evidence regime---full logs,
metrics, and traces---whereas real deployments are heterogeneous: some incidents
arrive with deep telemetry, others as little more than a terse textual report. A
method that works only when every signal is present is brittle precisely where
operations are hardest.

We present \sys, an agentic incident-triage system organized around retrieval
against a memory of resolved incidents. Given a window of observations
---telemetry when available, or a textual report when not---\sys retrieves the
most similar past incidents from the memory corpus and decides whether the
window warrants paging an engineer. Three ideas distinguish it. \emph{Hybrid
memory retrieval}: instead of trusting a single matcher, \sys fuses a dense
semantic retriever, a lexical retriever, and a knowledge-graph retriever built
over entities extracted from past tickets, combining their rankings so the
strengths of each cover the others' blind spots. \emph{Capability-gated
orchestration}: \sys first inspects what evidence a window actually carries and
runs only the reasoning steps that this evidence can support, so the same system
degrades gracefully from a full-telemetry pipeline to a lightweight text-only
one with no reconfiguration; costly steps are invoked only when cheaper ones
leave the decision uncertain. \emph{Incident-aware paging}: because a single
incident spans many consecutive windows, \sys tracks its recent decisions per
service and suppresses duplicate alerts, reporting roughly one page per incident
rather than one per window.

We evaluate \sys on three datasets of increasing realism: two
microservice-telemetry datasets we collect and release---built on the Online
Boutique and OpenTelemetry Demo applications---and a large real-world corpus of
38{,}642 resolved Apache Jira incidents across 24 projects, drawn from the
publicly available World of Logs collection~\cite{xiao2026worldoflogs}. On the real incidents---the hardest
and most consequential setting---hybrid retrieval places the correct prior
incident among its top five $97\%$ of the time, outperforming our own strong
dense retriever ($0.91$), classical lexical search ($0.73$), the graph retriever
alone ($0.31$), and a retrieval-augmented large-language-model baseline
($0.86$); every gap survives multiple-comparison correction. The end-to-end
agent reaches $0.96$ while its capability gating cuts per-window triage cost by
up to $78\%$ relative to running every step on every window, with no loss in
retrieval quality. Most striking is a controlled comparison between synthetic and
real data: the ticket-worthiness classification that dominates synthetic
benchmarks is so easy there that off-the-shelf classifiers are essentially
perfect (area under the precision--recall curve $\approx 1.0$), yet the same
task is no better than chance on real incidents. The real difficulty does not
lie in deciding \emph{whether} a window is an incident; it lies in retrieving
\emph{which} past incident it resembles---a finding that reframes how
operational-triage systems ought to be measured.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A capability-gated agentic triage architecture} that chooses its
  reasoning steps from the evidence available per window, escalates expensive
  steps only under cheap-path uncertainty, and suppresses duplicate pages across
  an incident's lifetime. The same system runs unchanged---and gracefully
  degraded---from full telemetry to text-only incidents (\S\ref{sec:approach}).

  \item \textbf{A hybrid memory-retrieval method} that fuses dense, lexical, and
  knowledge-graph signals and that, on real engineer-written incidents,
  significantly outperforms each constituent retriever, strong prior-art
  baselines, and a retrieval-augmented LLM (\S\ref{sec:results}).

  \item \textbf{A cross-setting empirical study} over synthetic and real data
  whose central finding is that triage \emph{classification} is largely an
  artifact of synthetic fault injection---saturated on synthetic data, at chance
  on real incidents---so that \emph{retrieval} is the transferable challenge; we
  further quantify what the agentic controller adds, isolating its cost savings
  at equal accuracy (\S\ref{sec:results},~\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

  \item \textbf{Two publicly released incident-triage datasets} that pair
  multi-signal observability telemetry---logs, metrics, distributed traces, and
  cluster events---with incident tickets and per-window ground-truth labels,
  across 27 and 49 controlled fault scenarios respectively. To our knowledge no
  existing public dataset couples operational telemetry with incident tickets at
  this granularity. We release them with all code, the evaluation harness,
  prior-art and LLM baselines, ablations, and multiple-comparison-corrected
  significance tests.
\end{itemize}
