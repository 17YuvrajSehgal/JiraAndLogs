\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern cloud services emit a relentless stream of alerts, and on-call engineers
must decide, often within minutes, which signal a genuine incident worth
investigating and which are noise~\cite{he2021survey,ghosh2022incidents}. The
decision is consequential both ways: a missed incident becomes an outage, while a
flood of low-value pages erodes trust in the alerting system itself. What makes an
on-call engineer effective is rarely raw signal-reading; it is \emph{memory}. Most
incidents rhyme with one the team has resolved before, and the fastest path from
symptom to fix is to recognize the situation as ``the message-broker outage from
last quarter'' and reuse the diagnosis that worked then. The institutional record
of past resolutions, typically a backlog of closed issue-tracker tickets, is
therefore the single most valuable triage aid a team owns, yet it is largely
untapped, because finding the \emph{right} prior incident for a live one is itself
a hard retrieval problem.

This need has motivated a growing body of work on automated incident triage and
AIOps~\cite{he2021survey,chen2019triage,ghosh2022incidents}, increasingly powered
by large language models (LLMs)~\cite{ahmed2023rootcause,chen2024rcacopilot}.
However, two assumptions limit how far these approaches carry into practice.

First, evaluation is conducted almost exclusively on \emph{synthetic}
fault-injection telemetry~\cite{chen2025aiopslab}: faults are scripted into
demonstration microservice applications, producing incidents that are clean,
balanced, and easily separable, and whether methods that excel under these
conditions transfer to the noisy, imbalanced, free-text incidents of real
operations remains largely unexplored. Progress is further hindered by the lack of
public datasets pairing operational telemetry such as logs, metrics, and traces
with incident tickets and ground-truth labels: existing resources offer telemetry
or tickets in isolation~\cite{zhu2023loghub,montgomery2022jira}, not the paired,
per-window-labeled corpus that triage evaluation requires.

Second, existing systems largely assume a fixed observability environment,
designing retrieval and reasoning pipelines for telemetry-rich
settings~\cite{ahmed2023rootcause,chen2024rcacopilot,zhou2024dbot}. Yet real
deployments are heterogeneous: some incidents arrive with detailed logs, metrics,
and traces, others as little more than a brief textual report. A triage system
should adapt its strategy to the evidence actually available rather than rely on a
fixed pipeline, a capability rarely considered or evaluated under realistic
conditions.

In this paper we present \sys, an adaptive incident-triage framework organized
around retrieval from a memory of resolved incidents. Rather than assuming a fixed
workflow, \sys dynamically selects retrieval and reasoning capabilities from the
operational evidence available for each incident, letting one framework span
heterogeneous regimes from telemetry-rich deployments to text-only reports. Its
design follows three principles: \emph{hybrid memory retrieval} combines dense,
lexical, and knowledge-graph similarity between live and historical incidents;
\emph{capability-aware execution} invokes only the retrieval, verification, and
evidence-gathering steps the available evidence supports and retrieval uncertainty
justifies, cutting computation while preserving quality; and \emph{incident-aware
paging} tracks evolving incidents across consecutive windows to suppress duplicate
pages.

To evaluate adaptability across evidence regimes and transfer from synthetic to
real deployments, we study \sys on three datasets of increasing realism: two newly
collected, publicly released microservice-telemetry datasets (Online Boutique and
OpenTelemetry Demo)\footnote{\url{https://zenodo.org/records/21082064}} and a large real-world corpus of 38,642 resolved Apache Jira
incidents across 24 projects from the World of Logs
collection~\cite{wang2026worldoflogs}. On the real incidents, the hardest setting,
hybrid retrieval places the correct prior incident in its top five $97\%$ of the
time, improving on each of its fused components in isolation (dense $0.91$, graph
$0.31$) and on external baselines (lexical $0.77$, retrieval-augmented LLM
$0.86$), every gap surviving multiple-comparison correction; the end-to-end system
reaches $0.96$ while capability gating cuts per-window cost by up to $78\%$ with no
loss in quality. Most striking is a controlled synthetic-versus-real comparison:
the ticket-worthiness classification that dominates synthetic benchmarks is
essentially perfect there (area under the precision--recall curve $\approx 1.0$)
yet no better than chance on real incidents. The difficulty is not deciding
\emph{whether} a window is an incident but retrieving \emph{which} past incident it
resembles, a finding that reframes how operational-triage systems ought to be
measured.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A capability-gated triage framework} that composes retrieval and
  reasoning steps to fit each incident's available evidence, running one workflow
  from full telemetry to text-only reports while cutting per-window cost through
  uncertainty-aware execution.

  \item \textbf{A hybrid retriever} fusing dense, lexical, and knowledge-graph
  signals that, on real engineer-written incidents, significantly outperforms
  every constituent retriever, strong prior-art baselines, and a
  retrieval-augmented LLM.

  \item \textbf{An empirical reframing} showing that the ticket-worthiness
  classification dominating synthetic benchmarks does not transfer to real
  incidents, identifying retrieval over incident memory as the challenge that
  does.

  \item \textbf{Two released telemetry-to-ticket datasets} pairing logs, metrics,
  traces, and cluster events with incident tickets and per-window labels (26 and
  52 fault scenarios) with, to our knowledge, no prior public dataset coupling
  telemetry and tickets at this granularity, alongside all code, baselines,
  ablations, and significance tests.\footnote{\url{https://zenodo.org/records/21083181}}
\end{itemize}
