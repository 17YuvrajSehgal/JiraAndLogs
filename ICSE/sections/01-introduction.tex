\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern cloud services emit a relentless stream of alerts, and on-call engineers
must decide, often within minutes, which signal a genuine incident and which are
noise~\cite{he2021survey,ghosh2022incidents}. The
decision is consequential both ways: a missed incident becomes an outage, while a
flood of low-value pages erodes trust in the alerting system. What makes an
on-call engineer effective is rarely raw signal-reading; it is \emph{memory}. Most
incidents rhyme with one the team has resolved before, and the fastest path from
symptom to fix is to recognize ``the message-broker outage from
last quarter'' and reuse the diagnosis that worked. The institutional record
of past resolutions, typically a backlog of closed issue-tracker tickets, is
therefore a team's most valuable triage aid, yet largely untapped: finding the
\emph{right} prior incident for a live one is itself a hard retrieval problem.

This need has motivated growing work on automated incident triage and
AIOps~\cite{he2021survey,chen2019triage,ghosh2022incidents}, increasingly powered
by large language models (LLMs)~\cite{ahmed2023rootcause,chen2024rcacopilot}.
However, two assumptions limit how far these approaches carry into practice.

First, evaluation is conducted almost exclusively on \emph{synthetic}
fault-injection telemetry~\cite{chen2025aiopslab}: faults scripted into
demonstration microservice applications produce incidents that are clean, balanced,
and easily separable, and whether methods that excel there transfer to the noisy,
imbalanced, free-text incidents of real operations remains largely unexplored.
Progress is further hindered by a lack of public datasets pairing telemetry (logs,
metrics, traces) with incident tickets and ground-truth labels: existing resources
offer telemetry or tickets in isolation~\cite{zhu2023loghub,montgomery2022jira}, not
the paired, per-window-labeled corpus triage evaluation requires.

Second, existing systems largely assume a fixed observability environment, building
retrieval and reasoning pipelines for telemetry-rich
settings~\cite{ahmed2023rootcause,chen2024rcacopilot,zhou2024dbot}. Yet real
deployments are heterogeneous: some incidents arrive with detailed logs, metrics,
and traces, others as little more than a brief text report. A triage system should
adapt to the evidence actually available rather than rely on a fixed pipeline, a
capability rarely evaluated under realistic conditions.

We present \sys (Adaptive Retrieval for Incident Support and Explanation), an
adaptive incident-triage framework organized around retrieval from a memory of
resolved incidents. Rather than assuming a fixed workflow, \sys selects retrieval
and reasoning capabilities from the evidence available for each incident, letting
one framework span regimes from telemetry-rich deployments to text-only reports. Its
design follows three principles: \emph{hybrid memory retrieval} (dense, lexical, and
knowledge-graph similarity between live and historical incidents),
\emph{capability-aware execution} (running only the steps the evidence supports and
uncertainty justifies), and \emph{incident-aware paging} (tracking evolving incidents
to suppress duplicate pages).

To test adaptability and synthetic-to-real transfer, we study \sys on three
datasets of increasing realism: two newly released microservice-telemetry datasets
(Online Boutique and OpenTelemetry Demo)\footnote{\url{https://zenodo.org/records/21096887}}
and 38,642 real Apache Jira incidents from 24 projects (World of
Logs~\cite{wang2026worldoflogs}). On the hardest, real incidents, hybrid retrieval
finds the correct prior incident in its top five $97\%$ of the time, beating every
fused component and external baseline (each gap surviving multiple-comparison
correction), while capability gating cuts per-window cost by up to $78\%$ at no loss
in quality. Most striking, the ticket-worthiness classification that dominates
synthetic benchmarks is near-perfect there yet no better than chance on real
incidents: the challenge is not \emph{whether} a window is an incident but
\emph{which} past one it resembles, reframing how triage ought to be measured.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A capability-gated triage framework} that composes retrieval and
  reasoning steps to each incident's available evidence, running one workflow from
  full telemetry to text-only reports while cutting per-window cost via
  uncertainty-aware execution.

  \item \textbf{A hybrid retriever} fusing dense, lexical, and knowledge-graph
  signals that, on real engineer-written incidents, significantly outperforms
  every constituent retriever, strong prior-art baselines, and a
  retrieval-augmented LLM.

  \item \textbf{An empirical reframing} showing that the ticket-worthiness
  classification dominating synthetic benchmarks does not transfer to real
  incidents, identifying retrieval over incident memory as the challenge that
  does.

  \item \textbf{Two released telemetry-to-ticket datasets} pairing logs, metrics,
  traces, and cluster events with incident tickets and per-window labels (26 and
  52 fault scenarios), the first public datasets to our knowledge coupling telemetry
  and tickets at this granularity, alongside all code, baselines, ablations, and
  significance tests.\footnote{\url{https://zenodo.org/records/21096898}}
\end{itemize}
