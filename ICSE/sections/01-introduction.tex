\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern cloud services emit a relentless stream of alerts, and on-call engineers must decide, often within minutes, which alerts signal a genuine incident
worth investigating and which are noise \textcolor{red}{[REF]}. The decision is consequential in both directions: a missed incident becomes an outage, while a flood of low-value
pages erodes attention and trust in the alerting system itself. What makes an on-call engineer effective is rarely raw signal-reading; it is
\emph{memory}. Most incidents rhyme with something the team has resolved
before, and the fastest path from symptom to fix is to recognize the current
situation as ``the message-broker outage from last quarter'' and to reuse the
diagnosis that worked then. The institutional record of how past incidents were
resolved (typically a backlog of closed issue-tracker tickets) is therefore
the single most valuable triage aid a team owns. Yet it is largely untapped,because finding the \emph{right} prior incident for a live one is itself a hard
retrieval problem.

This need has motivated a growing body of work on automated incident triage and
AIOps~\cite{he2021survey,chen2019triage,ghosh2022incidents}, increasingly powered
by Large Language Models (LLMs) ~\cite{ahmed2023rootcause,chen2024rcacopilot}, which
classifies alerts and incidents related to surfaces. However, two assumptions 
limit how far these approaches can be carried into practice.

First, the evaluation is conducted almost exclusively on
\emph{synthetic} fault-injection telemetry \textcolor{red}{[REF]}. Faults are scripted into
demonstration microservice applications, producing incidents that are clean,
balanced, and easily separable. Whether methods that excel under these
conditions transfer to noisy, imbalanced, and free-text incidents encountered
in real operations remains largely unexplored \textcolor{red}{[ref]}. Progress is further hindered by
the lack of publicly available datasets that pair operational
telemetry such as logs, metrics, and traces with incident tickets and ground-truth
labels, making reproducible comparisons difficult \textcolor{red}{[ref]}.

Second, existing systems largely assume a fixed observability environment, typically designing retrieval and reasoning pipelines for telemetry-rich environments \textcolor{red}{W: give examples with refs]}.
However, real operational deployments are inherently heterogeneous: some
incidents are accompanied by detailed logs, metrics, and traces, whereas others arrive as little more than a brief textual report. An operational incident-triage system should, therefore, adapt its retrieval and reasoning strategy to the
evidence actually available rather than relying on a fixed execution pipeline.
However, this capability is rarely considered or evaluated under realistic deployment
conditions.


In this paper, we present \sys, an adaptive incident-triage framework organized around retrieval from a memory of resolved incidents. Rather than assuming a fixed workflow, \sys dynamically selects retrieval and reasoning capabilities based on the operational evidence available for each incident, enabling the same framework to operate in heterogeneous evidence environments ranging from telemetry-rich deployments to text-only incident reports. The design is guided by three principles. First, \emph{hybrid memory retrieval} combines dense, lexical, and knowledge-graph retrieval to exploit complementary notions of similarity between live and historical incidents. Second, \emph{Capability-aware} execution invokes only those retrieval, verification, and evidence-gathering steps that are supported by the available evidence and justified by retrieval uncertainty, reducing unnecessary computation while preserving retrieval quality. Third, \emph{Incident-aware paging} tracks evolving incidents across consecutive windows to suppress duplicate pages and maintain consistent operational decisions.

To evaluate both adaptability across heterogeneous evidence regimes and transfer from synthetic to real deployments, we evaluate \sys on three datasets of increasing operational realism: two newly collected and publicly released microservice-telemetry datasets built on the Online Boutique and OpenTelemetry Demo applications, and a large real-world corpus of 38,642 resolved Apache Jira incidents spanning 24 projects from the publicly available World of Logs collection~\cite{wang2026worldoflogs}. On the real incidents (the hardest and most consequential setting) hybrid retrieval places the correct prior
incident among its top five $97\%$ of the time, outperforming our own strong
dense retriever ($0.91$), classical lexical search ($0.73$), the graph retriever
alone ($0.31$), and a retrieval-augmented large-language-model baseline
($0.86$); every gap survives multiple-comparison correction. The end-to-end
agent reaches $0.96$ while its capability gating cuts per-window triage cost by
up to $78\%$ relative to running every step on every window, with no loss in
retrieval quality. Most striking is a controlled comparison between synthetic and
real data: the ticket-worthiness classification that dominates synthetic
benchmarks is so easy there that off-the-shelf classifiers are essentially
perfect (area under the precision--recall curve $\approx 1.0$), yet the same
task is no better than chance on real incidents. The real difficulty does not
lie in deciding \emph{whether} a window is an incident; it lies in retrieving
\emph{which} past incident it resembles---a finding that re-frames how operational-triage systems ought to be measured.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{An adaptive incident-triage framework} that dynamically composes retrieval and reasoning capabilities according to the operational evidence available for each incident, enabling a single workflow to operate across heterogeneous observability regimes while substantially reducing computational cost through uncertainty-aware execution.

  \item \textbf{A hybrid memory-retrieval method} that fuses dense, lexical, and
  knowledge-graph signals and that, on real engineer-written incidents,
  significantly outperforms each constituent retriever, strong prior-art
  baselines, and a retrieval-augmented LLM.

  \item \textbf{A systematic empirical study} demonstrating that the ticket-worthiness classification task dominating synthetic incident-triage benchmarks does not transfer to real engineer-written incidents, identifying retrieval over historical incident memory as the transferable operational challenge.

  \item \textbf{Two publicly released incident-triage datasets} that pair
  multi-signal observability telemetry (logs, metrics, distributed traces, and
  cluster events) with incident tickets and per-window ground-truth labels,
  across 27 and 49 controlled fault scenarios respectively. To our knowledge no
  existing public dataset couples operational telemetry with incident tickets at
  this granularity. We release them with all code, the evaluation harness,
  prior-art and LLM baselines, ablations, and multiple-comparison-corrected
  significance tests.
\end{itemize}
