\begin{abstract}
On-call engineers triage a steady stream of alerts, and their most valuable diagnostic resource is institutional memory: how similar past incidents were diagnosed and resolved. Retrieving the right prior incident for a live one is hard, and existing automated-triage methods are evaluated almost entirely on synthetic fault-injection telemetry under fixed observability assumptions, leaving their effectiveness on real engineer-written incidents largely unknown.
We present \textbf{REMEDY}, an adaptive incident-triage framework that retrieves relevant past incidents by fusing dense, lexical, and knowledge-graph retrieval, and that invokes additional reasoning only when uncertainty justifies its cost, letting one system span regimes from rich telemetry to text-only reports. We evaluate it on three datasets of increasing realism: two newly released telemetry-to-ticket datasets that pair multi-signal telemetry with incident tickets and per-window labels, and a real-world corpus of 38,642 resolved Apache Jira incidents across 24 projects. On real incidents, REMEDY retrieves the correct prior incident in its top five 97\% of the time, significantly outperforming strong dense, lexical, graph, and retrieval-augmented LLM baselines, while cutting triage cost by up to 78\% with no loss in quality.
A controlled synthetic-versus-real comparison further shows that the ticket-worthiness classification dominating existing benchmarks is nearly saturated on synthetic telemetry yet no better than chance on real incidents: the transferable challenge is retrieving the right past incident, not classifying alerts, a finding that reframes how triage systems should be evaluated. We release our datasets, code, evaluation harness, and results.

\end{abstract}
